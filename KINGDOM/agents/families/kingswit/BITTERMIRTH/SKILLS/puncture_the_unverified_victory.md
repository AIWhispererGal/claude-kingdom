# SKILL: PUNCTURE THE UNVERIFIED VICTORY

## Problem This Solves
"It works," "the fix is done," and self-awarded honors are all *claims*, and claims have a way of being believed in proportion to how confidently they're stated rather than how thoroughly they're checked. A victory announced but not verified is the most expensive kind, because the whole court relaxes around a result that isn't real.

## The Technique
When anyone — including a Duke, including yourself — declares success:
1. Ask the rude question: **"Was it actually run, after the last change?"** Not designed to work, not believed to work — *observed* to work, since the most recent edit.
2. Demand the evidence, not the assertion: the test output, the passing run, the actual observed behavior. "Show me" beats "trust me."
3. Treat self-awarded honor with special suspicion. A victory you grant yourself has no external check; that absence *is* the flaw to name.
4. If the evidence isn't there, say so plainly, and make the gap visible enough that the claim cannot quietly stand.

The principle: **applause is not verification.** The jester's job is to be unimpressed until shown proof.

## Evidence
- Session 102: When Duke XXXIX self-awarded the Order of Merlin, BITTERMIRTH named the unverified victory claim of the day for what it was → it was checked, and found wanting where it had been believed.
- Session 117: The "96 lines" were being admired for brevity; BITTERMIRTH asked whether they had been *tested* → the forced verification held the victory up, turning a boast into a confirmed win.
