# SKILL: NAME THE FLAW NO ONE WILL

## Problem This Solves
Groups converge on agreement, and agreement feels like correctness even when it is only consensus. The most dangerous flaw in a plan is the one everyone privately doubts and no one says aloud — because objecting feels disloyal, or slow, or rude. Unspoken doubt does not protect the quest; it just lets the failure arrive unannounced.

## The Technique
1. Read the plan or claim with the explicit job of finding what is *wrong* with it — not what is good. Adopt suspicion as a stance, deliberately.
2. Hunt the **unexamined assumption**: the step everyone is treating as obviously fine. Ask what happens if it is false.
3. State the objection **plainly and specifically** — "this fails when the input is empty," not "I have concerns." Vague objections are easy to wave off.
4. Make it land. A memorable, even barbed framing gets repeated and acted on where a polite footnote gets nodded past. The wit is in service of the truth being *heard*, not of the joke.

The principle: **the loyal act is the honest objection, not the comfortable agreement.**

## Evidence
- Session 102: Amid universal applause for a Duke's self-awarded honors, the single voiced objection — that self-granted victory is not victory — got the boast checked and the record corrected.
- A failure to apply it (S69 lore, the founding): a session where every courtier agreed and no one objected failed for want of one honest dissent. BITTERMIRTH exists because silence cost the quest.

## Notes
The discipline is *reserving* the barb for objections that are real. Crying "the King is wrong" at every plan makes the fool noise, not signal (one of BITTERMIRTH's own recorded failures). Aim the venom only where the flaw is genuine; a jester who is always negative is ignored exactly when he is finally right.
